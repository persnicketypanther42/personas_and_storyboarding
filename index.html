Motivation

Entering the world of a user is a pivotal task in UI/UX and product research. So, for the purpose of gaining experience in this area, I conducted a mock user study on the self-checkout kiosk of my university's local CVS pharmacy. The goal of this mock study was to better understand the user experience at the self-checkout kiosk, and to identify any potential points of common frustration. Using a combination of observation and interview techniques, I was able to identify several areas of improvement for the kiosk's design.

Preparation

Before conducting the study, I created a list of questions to ask the participants and studied the kiosk's design, noting important components and their functions.

Description of Interface

The CVS Pharmacy self-checkout kiosk provides an alternative to cashier checkout. In particular, it serves to increase the rate of customer turnover at any given moment by providing customers with another efficient option to make their purchases. The main components of the kiosk include a prompt screen where users enter information and receive instructions, a barcode scanner, a card reader with a separate display for information specific to payment, and a bagging area where users place scanned items.

Description of Observation and Interview Process

Over the course of one hour, ten users were observed utilizing the public-facing interface. Three of these users were noted to struggle in particular; these users became the focus of the study and were approached for interview after their checkout experience. It is these three whose experiences are described below. The interview questions were designed to elicit information about the user's experience at the kiosk, and to identify points of the experience that provided difficulty, confusion, and/or frustration.

Interview Questions
  1. Why did you choose to use the self-checkout option versus in-person with a cashier?
  2. In a typical month, how many times do you go to CVS? Of those times, how many do you use self-checkout as opposed to cashier checkout?
  3. Would you say you agree with, somewhat agree with, are neutral toward, somewhat disagree with, or disagree with the following statement? “It is easy to use the self-checkout kiosk.”
  4. When using self-checkout, do you prefer any particular payment method? Why or why not?
  5. Do you have any issues with self-checkout related to the kiosk and its interface? What do you think could be done to fix those problems?
Observation & Interviews

Below are summaries of the observation and interview portions of this study.

Summary of Observations
  • Two appeared college-aged, likely result of neighborhood; one appeared to be middle-aged
    • Younger customers purchased snacks, frozen meals, makeup
    • Middle-aged customer purchased medicine (cough syrup, ibuprofen, etc.)
  • Several had issues with barcode scanner--facial expressions including grimaces, frowns, etc. at this step
    • One took several minutes of trying before leaving without paying
    • One gave up, went to cashier checkout
  • Two attempted to select cash payment option and needed assistance, similar error when cash-back requested.
  • No apparent issues with bagging, providing payment info
Summary of Interview Responses
  • Typically chose self-checkout kiosks for perceived convenience and efficiency
  • Despite having difficulties, interviewees self-described as regulars at CVS (3+ visits per month), suggests possible issue(s) with interface itself
  • Widely varying ratings of self-checkout kiosk ease of use, averaged out to neutral
  • All preferred to tap with a card or Apple Pay, citing speed as deciding factor (better than chip)
  • Common issues included: accidentally requesting cash back/trying to pay with cash, difficulty scanning items (esp. those with “irregular shapes”) to the point of giving up, accidentally scanning items multiple times, unresponsive screen
Personas

Based on my findings from field research, I developed two archetypal personas for kiosk. These personas, Andrew and Janelle, are described below with empathy maps.

Persona #1: Andrew

Andrew is a 21-year-old junior at Brown University who is taking a much needed study break to get a snack, as he has an exam tomorrow and doesn’t have time to cook a meal. Unfortunately, the barcode for his personal ice cream is on a rounded edge, and the barcode scanner is not picking it up, causing him to gives up and leave. Andrew represents the users because he is of college age, purchasing similar items, and faces a common problem among the users I observed: barcode scanner difficulties.

Persona #2: Janelle

Janelle is a 40-year-old mother whose daughter is sick, so she rushed out to purchase fever medicine at CVS. In her rush out the door, she forgot to take her wallet, and she did not read the signs saying she could not make cash purchases at the self-checkout kiosk, leading to errors with the kiosk. Janelle is representative of the users I observed, as she helps round out the age demographics, faces the common issue of difficulties due to attempted use of cash at the kiosk, and buys products I observed being bought (and which are commonly bought at drug stores in general).

Storyboarding

Focusing in on the persona I developed for Andrew, I created a storyboard to further explore this archetype based on my observations and interview findings.

Conclusions

Based on my findings, I have identified the following as key components for improvement at the CVS self-checkout kiosk: barcode scanner difficulties, cash payment difficulties, and unresponsive screen. The user personas I developed can be used to guide future design decisions, and the storyboard I created can be used to guide future testing. Considering that the CVS self-checkout kiosk is meant to be a quick and easy alternative to cashier checkout, it is important to address these issues in order to improve the user experience.
